

This assignment made me realize how much prompt quality actually affects the output. Initially, I assumed the model or UI would matter more, but it became clear that the **prompt is the most important part**. Once I added clear persona traits like tone, thinking style, and beliefs, the responses started feeling much more distinct — even though the same model was used underneath.

The idea of **GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out)** really stood out during this process. My early prompts were too simple, and all three personas sounded very similar. They were correct, but not unique. As I improved the prompts by adding more specific instructions and personality details, the outputs became more consistent and realistic. Few-shot style examples also helped a lot, because they showed the model how each persona should sound, not just what they should say.

Another important learning was how closely prompt design is tied to the overall product experience. For example, persona switching only worked properly when the chat history was reset — otherwise responses would mix styles. Also, handling API errors properly made the app feel much more reliable.

If I had more time, I would improve the research depth for each persona and add some way to test whether responses stay consistent in style. It would also be interesting to support longer conversations while keeping personas separate.

Overall, this assignment showed me that prompt engineering is not just writing instructions — it’s about designing a system where prompts, backend logic, and user experience all work together.
